Increasing rent in Ontario isn't as simple as sending your tenant a note that rent is going up next month. The *Residential Tenancies Act* sets out specific rules about how much you can increase, when, and how much notice you need to give. Miss any of these steps and the increase is unenforceable.

Here's everything you need to know.

## The Annual Rent Increase Guideline

For most residential tenants, Ontario caps rent increases each year at the **Rent Increase Guideline (RIG)** — a percentage set annually by the provincial government, tied to inflation.

The guideline applies to units that are **not exempt** from rent control. The 2026 guideline is **2.5%** (always verify the current year's figure at Ontario.ca — it changes annually).

You are not required to increase rent each year, but if you do, you cannot exceed the guideline for covered units without LTB approval.

## Which Units Are Subject to Rent Control?

This is where it gets nuanced.

**Rent control applies to:**
- Residential units first occupied for residential purposes **before November 15, 2018**

**Rent control does NOT apply to:**
- Units first occupied for residential purposes **on or after November 15, 2018**
- New purpose-built rental buildings completed after that date

If your unit was a new build or first rented after November 15, 2018, you can theoretically raise rent by any amount — though you still must follow the notice requirements described below. In practice, large above-guideline increases in newer units can still result in tenant turnover and vacancy costs that outweigh the gains.

If you're unsure when your unit was first occupied for residential purposes, the building permit date or first occupancy permit can help establish this.

## The 12-Month Rule

Regardless of rent control status, Ontario requires that **at least 12 months pass between rent increases**. You cannot raise rent twice in one year.

This clock starts from:
- The date the tenancy began, OR
- The last rent increase (whichever is more recent)

So if a tenant moved in on July 1 and their rent was $1,800, you cannot raise rent before July 1 of the following year — and even then, only with the required 90 days' notice.

## The 90-Day Written Notice Requirement

You must give your tenant **at least 90 days' written notice** of a rent increase before it takes effect. This applies to all residential tenancies regardless of rent control status.

The notice must:
- Be in writing
- State the new rent amount (not just the increase percentage)
- State the date the increase takes effect
- Use the **N1 form** (Notice of Rent Increase) available from the LTB

An email notice or a handwritten letter may not meet the legal requirements. Use the LTB's N1 form to be safe.

### How to Serve the N1

The N1 must be served by an approved method:
- In person to the tenant
- In the tenant's mailbox at the rental unit
- Under the door of the rental unit
- By courier

Email is not an approved service method unless the tenant has agreed to it in writing beforehand. SMS or verbal notice does not count.

Start your 90-day clock on the day after the notice is served, not the day it's sent.

## A Worked Example

Say your tenant pays $1,800/month and moved in before November 2018. You want to raise rent using the 2.5% guideline.

**New rent:** $1,800 × 1.025 = **$1,845/month**

You want the increase to take effect July 1, 2026.

- Count back 90 days from July 1: that's April 2, 2026.
- You must serve the N1 **on or before April 2, 2026**.
- If you serve it on April 3, the earliest your increase can take effect is July 2 — meaning you'd likely push to August 1 to hit a clean rent date.

Build in buffer. Serving on time feels easy until you're distracted in March and suddenly realize you've missed the window.

## Above-Guideline Increases (AGIs)

For units under rent control, landlords can apply to the LTB for an **Above-Guideline Increase** in certain circumstances:

- **Extraordinary operating costs:** Significant unexpected increases in property taxes, utilities, or security costs
- **Capital expenditures:** Major renovations or repairs that improve the building (e.g. roof replacement, window replacement, boiler replacement)

The AGI process involves filing an application with the LTB, providing financial evidence, and having it reviewed — often with tenant participation. It can take 12–18 months or longer to resolve. AGIs are granted in part, not always in full, and the increase may be phased in over time.

If you're considering an AGI, get professional advice before filing. The process is lengthy and not appropriate for minor repairs or routine maintenance.

## When a Unit Turns Over

When a tenant moves out and a new tenant moves in, you can set rent at any amount — regardless of rent control status or what the previous tenant paid. Once the new tenancy begins, the rent for that tenancy is locked in and the annual rules apply going forward.

This is why tenant turnover is often how landlords in older buildings adjust to market rates over time.

## Common Mistakes That Invalidate a Rent Increase

- **Giving less than 90 days' notice** — the increase simply doesn't apply
- **Using an informal note instead of the N1 form** — may not hold up if challenged
- **Calculating the increase incorrectly** — e.g. raising above the guideline on a rent-controlled unit
- **Raising rent twice in 12 months** — the second increase is void
- **Not serving notice properly** — an unserved or improperly served notice doesn't start the clock

If a tenant challenges your rent increase at the LTB and your paperwork has errors, the adjudicator will likely void the increase and require you to repay any overage you collected.

## Tips for Staying Organized

- **Set a calendar reminder** 120 days before each anniversary of the tenancy start date. This gives you time to decide whether to increase, prepare the N1, and serve it with buffer.
- **Keep a copy of every N1** you serve, along with a note of how and when it was served.
- **Calculate the exact new amount** — the N1 requires the dollar amount of the new rent, not just the percentage.
- **Check the guideline each year** — it changes annually, and going over it even slightly on a rent-controlled unit creates problems.
