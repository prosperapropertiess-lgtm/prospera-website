Rental income is taxable in Canada — but so is the cost of earning it. The CRA allows landlords to deduct reasonable expenses incurred to earn rental income, and many landlords leave real money on the table by not tracking these properly.

This isn't tax advice — for your specific situation, talk to an accountant. But this post will give you a solid foundation for what to track and what questions to ask.

## How Rental Income Is Taxed in Ontario

Rental income is reported on your personal tax return (T1) using **Form T776 (Statement of Real Estate Rentals)**. Net rental income — after eligible expenses — is added to your other income and taxed at your marginal rate.

Ontario landlords pay both federal and Ontario provincial income tax on net rental income. The combined marginal rates in Ontario range from about 20% at low income to over 53% at the top bracket. That makes deductions genuinely valuable.

## What You Can Deduct

### Mortgage Interest (Not Principal)

This is usually the largest deduction available. You can deduct the **interest portion** of your mortgage payments — not the principal repayment. Your lender will provide a mortgage interest statement each year.

If you took out a loan to purchase or improve the rental property, the interest on that loan is deductible. If you refinanced and used some of the proceeds for personal purposes, you can only deduct the portion of interest that relates to the rental property.

### Property Taxes

Your annual municipal property tax bill is fully deductible. In London, St. Thomas, and Strathroy, property taxes are due in installments — deduct what you actually paid in the tax year.

### Insurance Premiums

Landlord insurance (also called rental property insurance or dwelling fire insurance) is deductible. Standard homeowner's insurance typically doesn't cover rental properties, so you should have a dedicated policy anyway.

### Repairs and Maintenance

Money spent to keep the property in its current condition is deductible. This includes:

- Plumbing repairs
- Roof repairs (not replacement)
- Appliance repairs
- Painting
- Pest control
- Cleaning costs between tenancies

The key distinction the CRA makes is **repair vs. improvement**. Fixing a leaky faucet is a repair — deductible in the year it's paid. Replacing all the plumbing with upgraded pipes is an improvement — it must be capitalized and depreciated (more on that below).

### Property Management Fees

If you hire a property manager, their fees are deductible. This includes monthly management fees, tenant placement fees, and lease renewal fees. If you use a service like Prospera Properties, you can deduct those costs directly off your rental income.

### Advertising Costs

Listing fees on Kijiji, Facebook Marketplace, rental platforms, or any other advertising you pay for to find tenants is deductible.

### Professional Fees

Legal fees related to the rental property — such as drafting a lease, LTB representation, or collecting unpaid rent — are deductible. Accounting fees for preparing your T776 and rental income reporting are also deductible.

### Utilities Paid by the Landlord

If you pay utilities as part of the rental arrangement (heat, hydro, water), those costs are deductible. If utilities are in the tenant's name and they pay directly, you don't claim them (and you don't deduct them).

### Office Expenses and Travel

If you maintain a home office to manage your rental property, you may be able to deduct a portion of your home costs as an office expense — though this requires careful documentation and a dedicated workspace used exclusively for this purpose.

Travel to and from the rental property for maintenance, inspections, or tenant matters is deductible at the CRA's prescribed rate per kilometre. Keep a mileage log.

### Interest on Other Loans

If you borrowed money to make repairs or improvements to the rental property, the interest on those loans is deductible.

## Capital Cost Allowance (CCA): Tread Carefully

CCA is the CRA's version of depreciation — it lets you deduct a portion of the cost of the property and major improvements over time. On paper it looks attractive, but there's an important catch.

When you eventually sell the property, you'll face **recapture** — the CCA you've claimed over the years gets added back to your income in the year of sale. For most landlords, delaying that tax hit until sale is not worth the complexity.

More importantly: **the CCA deduction cannot create or increase a rental loss**. It can only reduce income to zero. So if your expenses already exceed your rental income, CCA won't help you further.

Talk to your accountant before claiming CCA.

## What You Cannot Deduct

- **Principal mortgage repayments** — only interest
- **Personal portion of expenses** for a property you also occupy
- **Land transfer tax and legal fees at time of purchase** — these are added to the adjusted cost base
- **Personal renovation projects** unrelated to the rental property
- **Expenses for a period when the unit was vacant and not listed for rent**

## Keeping Good Records

The CRA can audit rental property claims up to 4 years back (6 years for more serious issues). Keep:

- All receipts (digital or physical)
- Bank and credit card statements showing rental-related expenses
- Mortgage interest statements
- Property tax bills
- Insurance invoices
- Contractor invoices with details of work done
- Mileage logs
- Communication records with tenants

A simple spreadsheet or accounting app is fine for most landlords with one or two properties. If you're growing a portfolio, dedicated landlord accounting software or a bookkeeper will save you time at tax season.

## A Note on Part-Year Rentals

If you rented a property for only part of the year — say, you moved a family member in mid-year, or the property was vacant for several months — you can only deduct expenses for the period it was available to rent. Apportion carefully.

## Getting Your Numbers Right

Tax season surprises are usually bad surprises. The landlords who do best are the ones who track expenses throughout the year, not the night before their accountant appointment.

Whether you manage your own properties or work with a management company, keeping clear financial records is a basic habit that pays off every April. ## Frequently Asked Questions

### What rental expenses can an Ontario landlord deduct on their tax return?
Common deductible expenses include: mortgage interest (not principal), property taxes, landlord insurance premiums, repairs and maintenance, property management fees, advertising costs, professional fees (legal and accounting), utilities paid by the landlord, and mileage for property-related travel. All expenses must be documented with receipts and reasonably incurred to earn rental income.

### Can an Ontario landlord deduct the full mortgage payment as a rental expense?
No. Only the interest portion of your mortgage payment is deductible — not the principal repayment. Your lender provides an annual mortgage interest statement. If you used a portion of refinancing proceeds for personal purposes, only the interest attributable to the rental property portion is deductible.

### What is the difference between a deductible repair and a capital improvement for tax purposes?
A repair restores something to its previous condition and is deductible in the year it's paid — for example, patching a roof, repairing a leaky pipe, or repainting. A capital improvement adds value or significantly extends the useful life of the property — such as a full roof replacement, major electrical upgrade, or new addition. Capital improvements must be added to the property's adjusted cost base or depreciated using CCA, not deducted immediately.

### Can Ontario landlords deduct property management fees?
Yes. Property management fees — including monthly management fees, tenant placement fees, and lease renewal fees paid to a property management company — are deductible as expenses incurred to earn rental income. Keep invoices or statements from your property manager for CRA documentation purposes.

### How long should Ontario landlords keep receipts and tax records for rental properties?
Keep all rental income and expense records for at least six years from the end of the tax year they relate to, as the CRA can audit that far back for most situations. For capital improvements that affect your property's adjusted cost base, keep records until the property is sold and any resulting capital gains are reported.

When in doubt, always consult a qualified accountant who has experience with rental properties in Ontario. The rules are detailed and the stakes are worth getting right.
