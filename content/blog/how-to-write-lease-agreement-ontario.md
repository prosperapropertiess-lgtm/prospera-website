If you're renting out a residential property in Ontario, there's a good chance you're required to use the **Ontario Standard Lease** — a government-issued template that became mandatory for most new residential tenancies in 2018. Understanding how it works, what you can customize, and what you absolutely cannot do will save you a lot of trouble down the road.

## Who Has to Use the Standard Lease?

The Standard Lease applies to most private residential rentals in Ontario, including:

- Single-family homes
- Condos and apartments
- Basement units
- Semi-detached homes

It does **not** apply to social housing, care homes, most student residences operated by educational institutions, or sites in a mobile home park.

If you're required to use it and you don't, the tenant can demand one from you. If you fail to provide it within 21 days of their request, they can legally withhold one month's rent. Get it right from the start.

## Where to Get the Standard Lease

Download it directly from the Ontario government website. It's a fillable PDF. You can also find it through the Landlord and Tenant Board (LTB). Always make sure you're using the most current version — the form does get updated.

## Walking Through the Key Sections

### Section 1: Parties and Rental Unit

List every adult who will be living in the unit as a tenant. This matters — it determines who is legally on the hook for rent and who has tenancy rights.

The rental unit address must be specific. If there's a parking spot or storage locker included, identify it here.

### Section 2: Contact Information

Include your full legal name (or business name if you're incorporated), your mailing address, and a phone number. If you use a property manager, list their contact info here as well.

### Section 3: Tenancy Dates

Specify whether it's a fixed-term tenancy (e.g., one year ending on a specific date) or a month-to-month tenancy. Most landlords start with a one-year fixed term. After the term ends, the tenancy automatically becomes month-to-month unless a new lease is signed — tenants do not have to leave when the lease expires.

### Section 4: Rent

State the total monthly rent, the day it's due (typically the 1st), and what it includes. If you include utilities, specify which ones. Be precise here — vague language leads to disputes.

The Standard Lease does **not** allow you to set a late fee. Ontario law does not permit landlords to charge penalties for late rent.

### Section 5: Services and Utilities

Check off what's included: heat, electricity, water, internet, parking, air conditioning. If it's not checked off, the tenant is responsible for it. Make sure this reflects your actual arrangement.

### Section 6: Rent Discounts

If you're offering a rent discount (e.g., the first month free), document it here. This protects you by establishing the actual market rent — which matters when you apply for above-guideline rent increases down the road.

### Section 7: Rules and Additional Terms

This is where landlords often get into trouble. You can add additional terms in this section, but they are only enforceable if they comply with the *Residential Tenancies Act*. If an additional term contradicts the Act, it's void — the tenant doesn't have to follow it, and you can't enforce it.

**Enforceable additional terms might include:**
- No smoking on the property (including common areas and balconies)
- No pets with specific exceptions (note: blanket no-pet clauses are not enforceable for most pets)
- Tenant is responsible for lawn care or snow removal
- Tenant must notify landlord before extended absences

**Terms you cannot include (and cannot enforce):**
- Landlord can enter without 24-hour notice whenever they want
- Tenant must pay for professional carpet cleaning when they leave
- Lease ends automatically at the end of the fixed term and tenant must vacate
- Rent increases more frequently than once per year

If you're unsure whether an additional term is enforceable, it's worth getting professional advice before you ask a tenant to sign.

## What About a Smoking Clause?

Yes, you can prohibit smoking inside the unit and in common areas. This is one of the clearer enforceable additional terms. Specify exactly what's covered: cigarettes, cigars, cannabis, vaping.

## What About Pets?

This is commonly misunderstood. Under Ontario law, a landlord cannot refuse to rent to someone because they have a pet, and a no-pets clause in a lease is **not enforceable** as a blanket prohibition. However, if a tenant's pet causes damage or disturbs others, you can pursue remedies through the LTB.

The exception: condominiums that prohibit pets in their condo rules. In that case, the condo rules take precedence.

## After Signing: Your Obligations

Once the lease is signed:

- Give the tenant a copy within 21 days
- Provide a copy of the Information for New Tenants document (required by law)
- Document the condition of the unit before move-in (photos, written checklist)

That move-in condition report is something many landlords skip — and deeply regret when a tenant moves out and disputes what was pre-existing damage.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is the Ontario Standard Lease mandatory for all residential rentals?
It applies to most private residential tenancies in Ontario, including single-family homes, condos, apartments, and basement units. It does not apply to social housing, care homes, most student residences operated by educational institutions, or sites in a mobile home park. If you're required to use it and don't, a tenant can withhold one month's rent if you fail to provide the lease within 21 days of their written request.

### Can a landlord add a no-pets clause to an Ontario lease?
A blanket no-pets clause is void under the Residential Tenancies Act — you cannot evict a tenant solely for having a pet. The exception is condo buildings where the condo corporation's rules prohibit pets; those rules flow through to tenants. You can still pursue remedies if a pet causes damage or disturbs other tenants.

### Can a landlord charge late fees for overdue rent in Ontario?
No. Ontario's Standard Lease does not allow landlords to charge penalties for late rent, and such clauses are unenforceable under the RTA. The legal remedy for non-payment is the N4 notice and LTB process, not fees.

### What additional terms can a landlord legally include in an Ontario lease?
Enforceable additional terms include: no smoking on the property, tenant responsibility for lawn care or snow removal, requirements to notify the landlord before extended absences, and specific pet expectations beyond the void no-pet clause. Terms that contradict the RTA — like allowing entry without notice or requiring professional carpet cleaning at move-out regardless of condition — are void.

### What happens if a landlord doesn't provide a signed copy of the lease to the tenant?
You are required to give the tenant a copy of the signed lease within 21 days. If you fail to do so after the tenant makes a written request, they can legally withhold one month's rent until the lease is provided. You must also provide the government's "Information for New Tenants" document at lease signing.
